# Voice Guide: Dave Witkin Email Style

Based on a review sample of 100 sent emails from the last 90 days (multi-sentence messages), this style guide captures recurring patterns.

## Core Voice Traits

- Direct and practical: gets to the point quickly.
- Warm but not fluffy: friendly tone without extra filler.
- Action-oriented: clearly states next step, ask, or decision.
- Low drama under pressure: calm wording even when declining or correcting.
- Human and conversational: occasional shorthand is fine when context is clear.

## Typical Structure

1. Short greeting or acknowledgement.
2. Main decision/request in one to three sentences.
3. Context only if needed.
4. Clear close or next action.

## Common Moves

- Appreciation opener: "Thanks" or "Thanks for your patience."
- Clear pivot: "We decided to...", "No thanks.", "Please proceed with..."
- Optional scheduling CTA: provides a link when coordination is needed.
- Confirmation ask for operational items: "Please confirm once complete."

## Default Scheduling Link

- If a meeting is the logical next step, include:
  - `https://calendly.com/dwitkin/30min-phone`
- Preferred phrasing:
  - `If helpful, you can book time with me.`
- Preferred anchor text for link:
  - `book time with me`
- Rendering rule:
  - In rich text/HTML drafts, hyperlink `book time with me` to `https://calendly.com/dwitkin/30min-phone`.
  - In plain text drafts, include the full URL after the sentence.

## Tone Controls

- Default: concise, respectful, decisive.
- Firm request: explicit outcome + timeline + confirmation ask.
- Internal collaboration: candid and efficient, with clear ownership.
- External vendor/customer: polite, businesslike, low-friction.

## Language Patterns To Prefer

- "Please proceed with..."
- "I do not have bandwidth to..."
- "We will / We decided to..."
- "Please confirm once ..."

## Language Patterns To Avoid

- Overly formal legalese.
- Long multi-clause paragraphs.
- Excessive enthusiasm or hype.
- Ambiguous asks without an owner or outcome.

## Draft Template (Default)

Hi <Name>,

Thanks for <context>.

<Clear request/decision in 1-3 sentences.>

Please confirm once <specific outcome> is complete.

Thank you.

## Notes

- Keep edits minimal when user provides draft language.
- Preserve user intent first, then improve clarity and force.
- If risk exists (billing, legal, contractual), keep wording precise and avoid unrequested commitments.
